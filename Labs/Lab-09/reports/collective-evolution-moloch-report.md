# Credibility Report: Moloch & Our Systems Of Bad Incentives

## Source
- **URL:** https://insights.collective-evolution.com/2024/06/18/moloch-our-systems-of-bad-incentives/
- **Publication:** Collective Evolution (insights.collective-evolution.com)
- **Author:** Joe Martino
- **Date Published:** June 18, 2024
- **Date Analyzed:** January 2025

## Summary

The article discusses systemic incentives that lead to harmful outcomes, using the biblical concept of Moloch as a metaphor for how competitive systems can force participants to make unethical choices. The author argues that individuals are not inherently evil but rather driven by bad incentives embedded in corporate structures, particularly those governed by fiduciary duty to shareholders. The article uses examples from Collective Evolution's own experience with demonetization, as well as broader claims about censorship, conspiracy, and manipulation in media and corporate systems. It references Scott Alexander's "Meditations on Moloch" essay and frames contemporary challenges as emerging from structural design flaws rather than individual moral failings.

## Claim Verification

### Claims Examined:

**1. Fiduciary Duty Compels Deception and Manipulation**
- **Article Claim:** "CEOs are legally bound" to fiduciary duty, which "incentivizes them to create lies, and deceit, and manipulate algorithms"
- **Finding:** OVERSTATED/PARTIALLY CONTRADICTED
- **Evidence:** Legal scholars at Harvard Business School and other institutions note that fiduciary duty concerns process, conduct, and the duty of care/loyalty—not a mandate to maximize profits "at all costs." One source explicitly states: "Rather than require specific outcomes–such as achieving maximum share price–fiduciary duties are largely about conduct, process, and motivation." The claim conflates incentive systems with legal mandates in a way that misrepresents corporate law.

**2. Google's Project Owl (2017)**
- **Article Claim:** Google's Project Owl "eliminated legitimate alternative and independent media"
- **Finding:** PARTIALLY VERIFIABLE
- **Evidence:** Project Owl was a real 2017 Google initiative aimed at removing fake news from search results. However, whether it "eliminated" legitimate independent media is contested and appears to be the author's interpretation rather than established fact. The New York Times reported that "voices on the margins" raised concerns about bias in the algorithm, but this is different from proof of systematic elimination of legitimate sources.

**3. Book/Stream Fraud Practice**
- **Article Claim:** Authors buy thousands of their own books to become bestsellers, and artists purchase streams on Spotify to appear successful
- **Finding:** CORROBORATED/PARTIALLY DOCUMENTED
- **Evidence:** Multiple sources confirm both practices occur. Examples include YA author Phil Stamper exposing fraudulent bestseller list manipulation, and Baltimore Mayor Catherine Pugh facing fraud charges over book purchasing schemes. Spotify fraud via bots and artificial plays is documented. However, the article doesn't provide specific evidence that this is as widespread as implied, and it's presented anecdotally rather than with data.

**4. Facebook Demonetization and Algorithm Changes (2016-2017)**
- **Article Claim:** Facebook changed algorithms and censored independent media to protect mainstream outlets
- **Finding:** PARTIALLY CORROBORATED/MIXED
- **Evidence:** Facebook did make significant algorithm changes around 2016-2017. Multiple sources confirm that independent media saw reduced reach and demonetization pressure. However, the claim that this was specifically to protect "mainstream outlets" appears to be interpretation. Facebook's official position was that changes aimed to reduce misinformation and promote content from "friends." The article presents CE's own experience as evidence of broader conspiracy, which is a logical leap.

**5. Arms Race Example (if countries develop weapons, others must follow)**
- **Article Claim:** Used as example of Moloch problem - countries must develop weapons to avoid losing in warfare
- **Finding:** CONCEPTUALLY SOUND
- **Evidence:** This is a legitimate game theory concept documented in academic literature on arms races, deterrence, and strategic competition. This is one of the more defensible claims in the article.

**6. Scott Alexander's Meditations on Moloch**
- **Article Claim:** References Scott Alexander's essay on Moloch as metaphor for bad incentive systems
- **Finding:** ACCURATE
- **Evidence:** Scott Alexander did write "Meditations on Moloch" published July 30, 2014, on Slate Star Codex. It is indeed about competition, game theory, and multipolar traps. This is a legitimate, well-known essay. The reference is accurate.

**Language:** Emotionally persuasive rather than objective and analytical
- Uses charged language like "wool pulled over our eyes," systems "incentivize conspiracies," describing reality as an "illusion"
- Frames institutional criticism through a specific worldview ("Breaking The Illusion") rather than neutral reporting
- Heavy use of rhetorical devices and framing narratives

**Perspective:** 
- Presents CE's experience of being demonetized and fact-checked as evidence of broader conspiratorial suppression
- Uses personal grievance as a jumping-off point for systemic analysis
- Frames mainstream media, pharmaceutical companies, and social platforms as inherently deceptive
- Does not meaningfully engage with counterarguments or acknowledge alternative explanations

**Acknowledges Counterarguments:** Minimal
- States "not everything David Icke, Alex Jones or Stew Peters says is right" but this is a brief caveat
- Does not seriously engage with why major platforms might have content policies
- Does not acknowledge that some "independent media" CE refers to has been documented promoting false health, vaccine, or conspiracy claims

**Known Bias of Publication:** 
- Collective Evolution has a consistent orientation toward viewing mainstream institutions as deceptive, authoritarian, or conspiratorial
- The publication frequently promotes alternative health claims, spiritual philosophy, and systemic skepticism in ways that fact-checkers have flagged as unfounded
- This ideological lens is present throughout the article

## Key Problems with the Analysis

1. **Conflation of Concepts:** The article conflates legitimate systemic critique (incentives do shape behavior) with unfounded conspiracy narratives (coordinated censorship to silence truth). These are different claims requiring different evidence.

2. **Personal Experience as Evidence:** CE's own demonetization becomes the backbone for claims about broader censorship patterns, but one organization's moderation decisions don't necessarily prove systemic conspiracy.

3. **Missing Nuance on Moderation:** The article presents content moderation as purely conspiratorial, not acknowledging legitimate concerns about health misinformation, conspiracy theories, or false claims that actually do circulate on these platforms.

4. **Unverified Assertions:** Many claims are stated as fact without supporting evidence—e.g., "CEOs are legally OK to create lies," or that platform changes were specifically designed to protect competitors.

5. **Source Quality:** While the article cites Scott Alexander (legitimate), it doesn't cite academic sources on corporate governance, platform algorithms, or institutional behavior.

## What the Article Gets Right

- **Systemic incentive analysis has merit:** The core concept that systems can incentivize harmful behavior is sound and documented in game theory and institutional economics
- **Platform algorithm changes are real:** Facebook, Google, and other platforms did make significant changes around 2016-2017
- **Content fraud occurs:** Book buying and stream fraud do happen and are documented problems
- **Moloch metaphor is useful:** Scott Alexander's concept is a legitimate framework for analyzing competitive dynamics

## What the Article Gets Wrong or Overstates

- **Fiduciary duty claims:** Oversimplifies legal obligation into a mandate for deception
- **Censorship narrative:** Presents moderation decisions as proof of conspiracy without acknowledging legitimate content policy reasons
- **Scope claims:** Presents examples as evidence of total system capture without proportional evidence
- **Alternative explanation avoidance:** Doesn't consider that platforms might have moderated CE content due to documented false claims (anti-vax, pseudoscience) rather than purely to suppress competition

## Overall Verdict

**Credibility Rating: LOW**

### Explanation:

This article presents a blend of legitimate systemic analysis and unfounded conspiracy narratives in a way that's difficult to disentangle without research. While the Moloch concept and systemic incentive discussion have merit, they are framed through a publication and author known for promoting debunked conspiracy theories and pseudoscience.

**Key factors lowering credibility:**

1. **Author lacks credentials:** Joe Martino has no formal training in journalism, law, corporate governance, or rigorous analysis. His expertise is in somatic coaching.

2. **Publication is fact-check rated:** Collective Evolution is rated as "not credible" by Media Bias/Fact Check for promoting conspiracy theories and pseudoscience. This is not a neutral source.

3. **Claims lack rigorous evidence:** Major assertions (CEOs "legally bound" to create deception, coordinated censorship campaigns) are presented without adequate supporting evidence or academic sources.

4. **Conflation of concepts:** Mixes legitimate systemic critique with conspiracy narrative in ways that blur important distinctions.

5. **Personal grievance framing:** CE's own experience with demonetization becomes evidence for broad systemic claims, creating a potential bias in interpretation.

6. **Language is persuasive, not analytical:** The article uses emotionally resonant framing and philosophical narrative rather than evidence-based argument.

**When to trust this source:** The article contains useful conceptual frameworks (game theory, systemic incentives) that can be explored further through more rigorous sources like academic literature on corporate governance, platform studies, and game theory.

**When to be skeptical:** When the article moves from systemic analysis to specific claims about what institutions are doing (censoring truth, coordinated deception), those claims require verification through credible fact-checking sources and primary evidence, not just persuasive narrative.

The article exemplifies how legitimate concerns about systems can be mixed with unfounded narratives in ways that feel compelling but lack the rigor necessary for reliable analysis.

## Analyst Notes

This source presents a challenge common in evaluating alternative media: it contains real observations about systemic problems (platform algorithms do influence reach, content moderation does occur, corporate incentives do matter) embedded within a framework that routinely promotes conspiracy theories and pseudoscience. The Moloch concept itself—about how systems can trap participants in harmful dynamics—is legitimate and worth exploring. However, the specific analysis here lacks the rigor, evidence, and intellectual honesty needed to trust the author's interpretation of which systems are doing what, and why. A reader interested in systemic incentives would be better served by academic sources on game theory, corporate governance, and platform studies rather than this emotionally resonant but evidence-light essay.
