## Claim Verification

### Main Claim: "5G Causes Cancer"

**Verification Result:** **DIRECTLY CONTRADICTED by extensive scientific evidence**

#### Scientific Consensus:

Multiple authoritative organizations have extensively studied and debunked this claim:

1. **World Health Organization (WHO)**
   - States: "There is no evidence that electromagnetic fields cause health effects such as cancer, electrohypersensitivity, infertility or anything else."
   - Ongoing health risk assessment of 5G exposure

2. **U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)**
   - Conclusion: To date, the weight of scientific evidence has not effectively linked exposure to radio frequency energy from mobile devices with any known health problems

3. **Health Canada**
   - Finding: Based on available scientific evidence, there are **no health risks** from exposure to the low levels of radiofrequency EMF from cell phones, cell phone towers, antennas, and 5G devices

4. **International Commission on Non-Ionizing Radiation Protection (ICNIRP)**
   - Conclusion: Exposure to 5G at ICNIRP-recommended safety limits will not cause any harm

5. **Cancer Research UK**
   - Rating: "5G Causes Cancer" is listed as a **myth**
   - Explanation: While non-ionizing radiofrequency radiation is classified as "possibly carcinogenic" (Group 2B) by IARC, this classification means there isn't enough evidence to establish causation—not that it does cause cancer

6. **European Parliament Scientific Foresight Unit (2021)**
   - Finding: Epidemiological studies on MMW exposure at levels below ICNIRP occupational limits showed "little evidence of an association with any adverse health effects"

#### Scientific Basis:

- **5G uses non-ionizing radiation**, which does not damage DNA (unlike ionizing radiation such as UV light or X-rays)
- Current 5G exposure levels at 3.5 GHz are similar to existing mobile phone base station exposure
- 5G safety guidelines comply with ICNIRP standards established to limit exposure to safe levels

#### Related Studies:

- An Australian study (2018) found no link between increased mobile phone use and brain cancer incidence
- Multiple meta-analyses show no established causal link between mobile phone use and cancer risk
